The Strawberry Jam Lab

Turn fruit into jam — and discover why heat makes it set.

 ▶
The science: Water boils at 212°F. But jam isn't done until 220°F — eight degrees hotter! The sugar raises the boiling point, and tiny fibers in the fruit called pectin link together to trap the juice into a soft gel. That's why jam jiggles instead of pouring.

What you'll need

2 cups strawberries1 cup sugar1 Tbsp lemon juicePotCooking thermometerClean jarSpoon

Build it together Grown-up at the stove

  1. Wash and mash the strawberries in the pot. Notice how much juice comes out!
  2. Stir in the sugar and lemon juice. (The lemon adds acid that helps it set and keeps it safe.)
  3. Heat slowly, stirring, until it bubbles. Clip on the thermometer.
  4. Watch the number climb. When it reaches 220°F, the jam is at its “gel point.”
  5. Let it cool, spoon into a clean jar, and keep it in the fridge.

Concepts: boiling point, measurement, food safety & acidity, states of matter.

🪱 Flagship project · the one you asked for

Worm-in-a-Cup Observatory

Take the worm farm home — a see-through worm habitat kids can watch all week.

 ▶
The science: Worms are nature's recyclers. They eat food scraps and turn them into castings — the richest plant food there is. Their tunnels also let air and water reach plant roots. In a clear cup, kids can actually see the tunnels appear day by day.

What you'll need

Clear plastic cup or jarSoilSand2–3 red wiggler wormsSmall food scrapsDark paperSpray bottle of water

Build it together

  1. Layer soil, then a thin line of sand, then more soil. The sand lines let you spot the tunnels.
  2. Add 2–3 red wigglers on top and a few tiny food scraps (fruit or veggie peels).
  3. Lightly mist with water so it's damp like a wrung-out sponge — never soaking.
  4. Wrap the cup in dark paper. Worms like the dark; they'll tunnel along the glass where you can peek.
  5. Each day, unwrap and look: new tunnels? Scraps shrinking? Draw what you see.
  6. After about a week, gently return the worms to a garden or compost bin. Thank them!

Concepts: decomposition, ecosystems, soil health, observation & journaling.

Grow a Plant from a Kitchen Scrap

No garden needed — regrow a green onion or lettuce on the windowsill.

 ▶
The science: Many plants store energy in their base and can grow all over again. Put the root end in water and it “wakes up” — sending out new roots and leaves. It's the same regrowing power that fills a whole garden bed.

What you'll need

Green onion, lettuce, or celery baseSmall glass or jarWaterA sunny windowsillRuler

Build it together

  1. Save the root end (about 2 inches) when a grown-up trims the vegetable.
  2. Stand it root-down in a glass with just enough water to cover the roots.
  3. Put it on a sunny windowsill. Change the water every other day so it stays fresh.
  4. Measure the new growth with a ruler each day and write it down.
  5. In about a week, plant it in soil — or snip the new green onion right into dinner!

Concepts: plant life cycle, roots & regrowth, what plants need, measuring change over time.

Build a Bee & Butterfly House

Make a cozy home that brings real pollinators to your yard.

 ▶
The science: Not all bees live in big hives — most are gentle solitary bees that nest alone in little tubes. Bees and butterflies are pollinators: they move pollen from flower to flower so plants can make fruit and seeds. No pollinators, no strawberries! A bee house gives them a safe place to rest and nest.

What you'll need

Clean tin can or small boxPaper straws or hollow bambooScissorsStringA few flowers nearby

Build it together Grown-up cuts

  1. Cut the straws or bamboo so they fit the depth of the can — these are the bee “rooms.”
  2. Pack them in tightly, open ends facing out, until the can is full.
  3. Tie a string around the can to make a hanger.
  4. Hang it on a sunny fence or tree, near flowers, about chest height.
  5. Watch quietly over the weeks — see who moves in! Keep a pollinator journal.

Concepts: pollination, solitary bees vs. hives, habitats, observing wildlife.

🌐 Bonus project

Build Your First Web Page

Make a one-page website about your garden — real code, kid-sized.

 ▶
The idea: A website is just a text file the computer knows how to draw. With a few simple tags, your child can publish a page about what they grew — and see their own words become a real web page.

What you'll need

Any computerA free account on a site like Neocities or GlitchA grown-up to help sign up

Build it together

  1. Make a free account and start a new page.
  2. Type a big title with <h1>My Garden</h1>.
  3. Add a sentence about your favorite plant inside <p> </p>.
  4. Add a photo of your garden with an image tag.
  5. Click publish — you made a real website! Share the link with family.

Concepts: how websites work, sequencing & structure, digital creativity.
